How MTA-STS Policy Fetch Works (and Breaks Deliverability)

MTA-STS stands for Mail Transfer Agent Strict Transport Security. Think of it like HTTPS for email handoffs between servers. When you publish a special DNS entry on your sending domain, receiving servers like Outlook's check it to grab a policy file from your HTTPS site. That file declares which of your mail servers must use TLS encryption—no exceptions.

The fetch process is picky. Receiving servers look up a specific subdomain TXT record on your domain, which points to the exact URL of your policy file, includes a unique timestamp to track changes, and lists a digital fingerprint of the file itself. They download that file securely, verify the fingerprint matches, and confirm your mail servers are covered. If any step fails—a bad URL, unsecure HTTPS, mismatched fingerprint, or even a temporary server glitch—Outlook now rejects the email with a permanent bounce code signaling TLS failure.

This isn't theoretical. Senders with the DNS entry but broken policies see emails drop like stones in Outlook and Hotmail inboxes. It's like locking your front door but leaving the key under the mat—looks secure, but fails the real test.

Auditing Your Setup: Tools and Common Failure Modes

Start with free online testers that mimic how Outlook fetches your policy. They probe your DNS for the right subdomain entry, chase the HTTPS link, check the file's contents against its listed fingerprint, and flag if your mail exchangers aren't fully covered. Look for A-grade results—no warnings on accessibility, security, or mismatches.

Common pitfalls? DNS records missing the version tag, a fresh timestamp, or testing mode flag. Policy files served over plain HTTP instead of HTTPS, or with wrong hashes because the file changed post-publication. Even tiny syntax slips in the file—like malformed mail exchanger declarations—cause 404 errors during fetch.

  • Probe your MTA-STS subdomain with MXToolbox and CheckMTASTS.com for full validation.
  • Scan Google Postmaster Tools for TLS reputation dips and Microsoft SNDS for bounce trends.
  • Confirm your ESP supports modern TLS versions on their status pages.
  • Match policy file fingerprint exactly in DNS—no edits after hashing.
  • Start in testing mode to avoid self-inflicted rejections during setup.

Step-by-Step Fixes for TXT Records and Policy Files

Getting this right takes deliberate stages, but it's straightforward if you follow order. Don't rush to enforcement—test first, or risk blocking your own mail.

  1. Publish TXT RecordAdd the entry on your MTA-STS subdomain in testing mode. It announces your intent, points to the policy URL, stamps a unique ID based on publication time, and includes the file's security hash.
  2. Deploy Policy FileUpload a plain text file to your site's well-known folder via HTTPS. List version, mode, and all mail exchanger patterns with their fingerprints.
  3. Validate and TestRun simulators repeatedly. DNS propagation takes 48 hours, so monitor over days and tweak as needed.
  4. Enforce and MonitorFlip to enforce mode once clean. Watch inboxes and dashboards for a week—no surprises.
  5. Aggregate ReportingSet up reports from receivers to track real-world compliance long-term.
